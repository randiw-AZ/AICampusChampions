## Reading Guide

### Reading 1: [Experimental Evidence on the Productivity Effects of Generative AI](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/inline-files/Noy_Zhang_1_0.pdf) (Noy & Zhang, 2023\)

What to look for as you read:

* The experimental design: who were the participants, what tasks did they perform, how was 'quality' measured?  
* The 37% time reduction and quality improvement findings — but also WHICH workers benefited most and why.  
* The finding that lower-skilled workers benefited MORE from AI access than higher-skilled workers — what does this suggest about the distributional effects of AI?  
* The limitations the authors themselves acknowledge — what can and cannot be generalized from this study to your work context?

Guided reading questions (annotate answers as you go):

* Q1: What type of tasks were used in the Noy & Zhang experiment? Are these tasks representative of the most cognitively demanding work in your professional role?

Answer: Writing press releases, short reports, analysis plans, and delicate emails. No, these are low-cognitive-demand tasks. 

* Q2: The study found that AI 'compressed' the skill distribution — lower-skilled workers improved more. What are the institutional implications of this finding for how AI tools are deployed in higher education staff contexts?

Answer: Students at the lower end of the scale may benefit most from using AI? 

* Q3: The paper found quality improvements in addition to time savings. What metric was used to assess 'quality,' and is that metric applicable to the documents you produce?

Answer: Reviews by blinded experienced professionals working in the same occupation. Yes. 

* Q4: What is one methodological limitation of this study that would affect your confidence in applying its findings to your specific work context?

Answer: Lack of context-specific knowledge or precise factual accuracy. Context and factual accuracy are vital for differential diagnosis and clinical reasoning. 

Key terms — write your own definition after reading:

| Term | Your definition (In your own words) |
| ----- | ----- |
| Randomized controlled trial (RCT) | Gold-standard study of design; participants are randomly assigned to either an experimental (treament) group or a control group (placebo); it’s double-blinded if the evaluators don’t know which participants are in the experimental or control group |
| Treatment effect | Impact of treatment (using Chat-GPT) on outcome |
| Skill compression | Reduction in inequality between workers when using generative AI (i.e., using generative AI minimizes the task performance gap, especially for low-skilled workers) |
| Task completion time | How long it takes to complete a task with reasonable (i.e., satisfactory) proficiency |
| Output quality assessment | Evaluation of output by experienced professionals |

### Reading 2: [Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4573321) (Dell'Acqua et al., 2023\)

What to look for as you read:

* The 'jagged frontier' definition: AI performs well on tasks INSIDE its frontier, poorly on tasks OUTSIDE — but the boundary is irregular and non-obvious.  
* The 'centaur' vs. 'cyborg' collaboration styles identified in Section 5 — which best describes how you used AI in your Module 2 tasks?  
* The inside-the-frontier vs. outside-the-frontier performance differences — what tasks in YOUR professional context sit inside vs. outside?  
* The authors' discussion of over-reliance: why did consultants who used AI on outside- frontier tasks perform WORSE than those who didn't use AI?

Guided reading questions (annotate answers as you go):

* Q1: What is the 'jagged frontier' and why is it 'jagged' rather than a smooth capability boundary?

The jagged frontier is the uneven impact of AI capabilities, where AI improves performance for some tasks and worsens it for others. 

* Q2: How did Dell'Acqua et al. identify which tasks were 'inside' vs. 'outside' the frontier experimentally? What implications does this classification method have for practitioners who cannot run experiments?

They collaborated with Boston Consulting Group to determine what tasks were on this boundary for complex and knowledge-intensive work. Inside the frontier tasks included creativity, analytical skills, persuasiveness, and writing. Outside the frontier tasks included problem-solving tasks using quantitative data, customer and company interviews, and persuasive writing. 

* Q3: What does the 'centaur' collaboration style mean in this paper? What does the 'cyborg' style mean? Which is more appropriate for the task you will complete in Task 2?

Centaur and cyborg are not explicitly used in this paper. I had to look up the distinction between them, and it is a subtle distinction. Centaur seems to be a give-and-take collaboration, where cyborg is more integrated. I’m not quite sure what the latter means, so I suspect I will be using the centaur style. 

* Q4: The paper found performance degradation for AI-assisted workers on outside- frontier tasks. What cognitive mechanism do the authors propose to explain this? Do you find the explanation convincing?

Performance degradation for tasks that are outside the frontier is referred to as “Falling asleep at the wheel”. Because AI performs exceptionally well on tasks within its "jagged frontier," workers may become complacent and fail to notice when a task actually falls outside those capabilities, leading them to accept flawed or incorrect AI output. I might call this cognitive offloading or cognitive surrender. It’s important to keep the human in the loop for these tasks. 

Key terms — write your own definition after reading:

| Term | Your definition (In your own words) |
| ----- | ----- |
| Jagged technological frontier | The boundary between what AI can (inside the frontier) and cannot (outside the frontier) do. This boundary changes as models expand and change. Humans who can skillfully navigate this boundary are at an advantage.  |
| Centaur collaboration | **Centaurs:** Divide the labor between the human and the AI. Much like the mythological creature that is half-human and half-horse, a "Centaur" worker switches between tasks, performing some themselves and delegating others to the AI based on which is better suited for the specific task. |
| Cyborg collaboration | **Cyborgs:** Weave AI into every stepe, where the human and AI work together on the same task simultaneously. The human and machine efforts are blended together throughout the process, much like a biological organism with integrated technological components. |
| Over-reliance | Knowledge workers trust AI output too much, failing to recognize its limitations and providing insufficient human supervision for tasks where the AI is actually incapable.  |
| Inside-frontier task | AI can complement and improve human work |
| Outside-frontier task | AI output is inaccurate, less useful, and can degrade human performance |

## Peer Participation Guide & Self-Assessment Rubric

### Peer Participation Guide

What to post in the course forum:  
Post one concrete finding from your Task 2 Completion Log: what did AI do well, what did it get wrong, and what specific edit you made to correct or improve it. Do not include the full document — one specific example is enough.

How to respond to peers:  
Read your peer's finding and respond with one sentence that connects their experience to either Noy & Zhang (2023) or Dell'Acqua et al. (2023). For example: 'Your experience with \[specific finding\] illustrates the jagged frontier concept — \[specific connection\].'

Discussion prompts to deepen the conversation:

* Looking at your Workflow Audit matrix: did your highest data-sensitivity tasks also have high AI integration potential? What does that tension mean for your institution?  
* Did your Task 2 documentation show you operating as a 'centaur' or a 'cyborg' (Dell'Acqua et al.)? Which style do you think is more appropriate for your professional context — and why? \*In your Scenario Analysis, did you find any scenario where your institution's current policy was clearly silent? What would you need a policy to say to make that scenario navigable?

Forum etiquette for this module:

* Do not share full documents from Task 2 in the forum — they may contain institutional or sensitive content. Share specific, anonymized examples only.  
* When connecting a peer's experience to a paper's finding, cite the paper specifically (author, year, concept name) rather than paraphrasing vaguely. This models the reference practice AI Champions use.
