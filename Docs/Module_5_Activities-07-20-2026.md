### Reading 1: [NIST Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0)](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/NIST.AI.100-1.pdf) Core (2023)

|  | Description |
| ----- | ----- |
| Citation: | National Institute of Standards and Technology (2023). Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0). NIST AI 100-1. U.S. Department of Commerce. [https://doi.org/10.6028/NIST.AI.100-1](https://doi.org/10.6028/NIST.AI.100-1) |
| Assigned section | The 2-page AI RMF Core (the GOVERN / MAP / MEASURE / MANAGE functions), Fig 5 in Section 5, pp 20-21. If the image in not clear enough, you can browse the descriptions of each function that follow (skipping Tables 1-4). There is an online version of the [AI RMF Core](https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/) if interested, and you can also find some [Use Cases Examples](https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/usecases/) |
| Estimated reading time | \~20 minutes (document is dense — read slowly) |
| Why this matters | The NIST AI RMF is the primary U.S. federal framework for organizational AI risk management. It is sector-neutral and designed to be adaptable to universities. Understanding its four-function structure (GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE) gives you a vocabulary for evaluating institutional AI policies — and identifying when a policy covers only some functions while ignoring others. |

What to look for as you read:

* The four GOVERN-MAP-MEASURE-MANAGE functions: what is each function responsible for? Note that GOVERN provides the organizational foundation; the other three operate iteratively on specific AI systems.  
* The distinction between 'AI risks' and 'AI trustworthiness properties' — NIST lists properties including accuracy, explainability, fairness, privacy, reliability, safety, security, and transparency. Which of these does your institution's current AI guidance address?  
* The concept of 'AI actors' — NIST identifies multiple roles (designers, developers, deployers, users, affected parties). Which actor role best describes your professional relationship to AI tools at your institution?  
* What the framework does NOT specify: NIST intentionally does not prescribe specific technical solutions or mandate particular governance structures. This principles-based approach has trade-offs — identify one advantage and one limitation.

Guided reading questions — annotate answers as you go:

* Q1: The NIST AI RMF organizes risk management into four functions: GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE. What does each function cover, and what happens in an organization that has only implemented GOVERN (written policies) without MAP or MEASURE?

Answer: The **Govern** function includes a culture of risk management in an organization that outlines guidelines that anticipate, identify, and manage the risks. The **Map** function establishes a context to frame risks. The **Measure** function assesses, analyzes, and tracks identified risks. The **Manage** function prioritizes and acts upon risks based on anticipated impact. When an organization implements only Govern without Map or Measure, it cannot determine the impact and effectiveness of its guidelines. 

* Q2: NIST lists seven trustworthiness properties for AI systems. Which three are most relevant to AI used in student-facing services at a university, and why?

Answer: The three most relevant trustworthiness properties for AI systems are: 1\) **Privacy-Enhanced**, 2\) **Fair \- with Harmful Bias Managed**, and 3\) **Secure and Resilient**. Privacy-Enhanced is important to protect anonymity and confidentiality of information. Fair is important to prevent bias and discrimination. Secure and resilient is important to maintain functionality and protect against unauthorized access or attacks. 

* Q3: The NIST framework is 'voluntary' in the U.S. context — there is no current federal law requiring its adoption. What incentive structures (accreditation, grant requirements, litigation risk) might effectively make it de facto mandatory for research universities?

Answer: Incentive structures such as accreditation, grant requirements, and litigation risk may push adoption. 

* Q4: How does the NIST AI RMF's definition of 'AI risk' compare to the way your institution currently discusses AI risk in its communications or policies? What risk categories does your institution address? What does it miss?

Answer: The NIST AI RMF’s definition of risk includes the ways AI technologies can negatively impact individuals, groups, organizations, communities, society, the environment, and the planet. The risk categories my institution addresses are individuals and groups (students, faculty, and staff), and groups and organizations (e,g., College of Medicine). It misses some of the broader categories such as communities, society, the environment, and the planet.

### Reading 2: [UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence](https://unesdoc.unesco.org/ark:/48223/pf0000381137) — Executive Summary (2021)

|  | Description |
| ----- | ----- |
| Citation | Unesco. (2022). Recommendation on the ethics of artificial intelligence. United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization. |
| Assigned section | Preamble (3 pages) \+ Section III Values and Principles (pp. 18-23 of full document, optional but recommended). |
| Estimated reading time | \~20 minutes |
| Why this matters | The UNESCO Recommendation is the first global normative instrument on AI ethics, adopted by all 193 UNESCO member states in November 2021\. Unlike NIST (which is operational and sector-neutral), UNESCO grounds AI governance in human rights principles and addresses cross-border, equity, and environmental dimensions that the NIST RMF does not. Together, the two frameworks provide complementary governance perspectives. |

What to look for as you read:

* The ten core values UNESCO identifies: human rights and human dignity, living in peaceful just and interconnected societies, protecting the environment and the ecosystem, ensuring diversity and inclusiveness, right to privacy and data protection, rule of law and accountability, transparency and explainability, responsibility and accountability, ethics and human oversight of AI, and promotion of sustainable development. Note which values align with and which extend beyond NIST'strustworthiness properties.  
* The UNESCO concept of 'right to privacy' as a fundamental precondition for other rights — how does this framing differ from treating privacy as one property among several?  
* The explicit attention to 'multi-stakeholder participation' in AI governance — UNESCO calls for involvement of civil society, marginalized communities, and young people in AI policy-making. What does this imply for university AI governance structures?  
* The equity and environmental justice dimensions — UNESCO specifically addresses AI's disproportionate environmental costs and the risk that AI benefits accrue primarily to wealthy nations and wealthy institutions. Are these concerns addressed in your institution's current AI guidance?

Guided reading questions — annotate answers as you go:

* Q1: The UNESCO Recommendation is legally non-binding but was adopted unanimously by 193 member states. What does this status imply for its authority relative to the EU AI Act (legally binding) and the NIST AI RMF (voluntary U.S. framework)?

Answer: The UNESCO Recommendation is a standard-setting blueprint for both the European Union and the United States. The EU has implemented legally binding rules. The United States adoption is voluntary and subject to political changes. 

* Q2: UNESCO grounds its framework in human rights law rather than technical risk management. How does this rights-based framing change the governance questions a university would need to ask before deploying an AI system in student advising?

Answer: A university must ask if the AI objectifies students or undermines their autonomous decision-making. Students should have the right to request explanations and challenge AI-informed outcomes, and mandate that AI should never replace human responsibility. 

* Q3: Compare UNESCO's list of core values with NIST's trustworthiness properties. What does UNESCO address that NIST does not? What does NIST address that UNESCO treats only implicitly?

Answer: UNESCO addresses Human rights and dignity as foundations, the environment as a core value, policy recommendations for gender, culture, education, and health, global solidarity and equity, and psychological and relational impacts. NIST addresses technical performance properties, and makes a greater distinction between explainability and interpretability. 

* Q4: UNESCO calls for 'meaningful human oversight' of AI systems as a core principle. What does 'meaningful' oversight require in a university context — what mechanisms would make oversight more than a formality?

Answer: UNESCO specifically mandates that AI never replace ultimate human responsibility. The university should provide designated staff to oversee and potentially override the AI outcomes. The university should also provide auditability and traceability of algorithms. Students and marginalized groups should be active participants in governance.

| Human rights-based AI governance | Grounds the entire AI lifecycle in international human rights law and human dignity |
| Right to privacy (UNESCO framing) | Protection of human dignity, autonomy, and agency |
| Meaningful human oversight | AI systems never replace ultimate human responsibility and accountability |

### Task 3: AI Policy Position Paper — 300 Words \[30 min\]

| Purpose | Develop the capacity to formulate and argue for a reasoned, evidence- grounded position on a contested AI policy question — the core communication skill for AI Champions advising institutional governance bodies. |
| :---- | :---- |

Step-by-step instructions:

1. Select one of the three contested AI policy questions from the Module 5 Scenario Set in the course platform:  
   (A) 'Should universities require mandatory disclosure of ALL AI use that substantively shaped a student assignment's content or argument?',  
   (B) 'Should AI-generated text be eligible for listed authorship credit in scholarly publications?',  
   or (C) 'Should universities restrict which AI vendors faculty may use with student data, even at the cost of limiting pedagogical innovation?'  
2. Write your position paper in the structured template below — do not write as free prose until you have completed the outline.  
3. Sentence 1 (Opening / Thesis): State your position as a clear, falsifiable claim. Someone should be able to disagree with it.  
4. Evidence Block 1: Cite one paper or policy from this workshop with a specific finding that supports your position.  
5. Evidence Block 2: Cite a second source (paper, policy, or institutional data) with a different type of support.  
6. Counterargument: State the strongest version of the opposing position — the version that is hardest to dismiss.  
7. Rebuttal: Explain why your position is more defensible despite the counterargument — identify a scope condition, a competing value, or evidence the counterargument relies on.  
8. Closing Sentence: Restate your position in light of the argument you have made.  
9. Total: 280–320 words. Do not exceed 320 words.

| (C) Vendor restrictions |

| ----- |

| Guidance Hints |
| ----- |
| \* The KEY DEFINITIONS section is where most policies fail. 'AI-generated content' and 'AI-assisted content' require different governance responses: the former implies AI produced the core substance with minimal human input; the latter implies human-directed creation with AI support for specific steps. If your guideline does not distinguish these, every subsequent provision becomes ambiguous. |
| \* RESTRICTED USES should reference risk categories from the NIST AI RMF (data sensitivity, lack of explainability, high-stakes decisions) rather than naming specific tool brands. Brand-based restrictions become outdated within months; risk-based restrictions remain valid as tools evolve. |
| \* The Review Date is not bureaucratic formality — it is governance integrity. Any AI use guideline published in 2024 without a review date within 12 months will almost certainly be outdated before participants have adopted it consistently. Set the date; assign the role responsible for the review. |
| \* Write for your actual audience. A guideline for a research lab using sensitive data has very different requirements from one for a writing-intensive course or a student services office. Resist the temptation to write a general-purpose document — specificity is what makes a guideline actionable. |
